The most important resources in an institution include information resources, as well as human resources, and financial resources. The information available to an organization must be accessible, reliable, and available to effectively support decision-making processes. Information Management (IM) is the discipline that guides and assists an institution to effectively manage its information, from planning to disposal and/or long-term preservation. DFO IM manages the planning, capturing (creation), acquisition, organization, use and storage, preservation, protection, and disposal of government information.

DFO's Information Management teams provide the following services:

  • Training
  • Management of records and electronic documents
  • Management of records and paper documents
  • Advisory and support services for information management projects

“Information management is a discipline that directs and supports effective and efficient management of information in an organization, from planning and systems development to disposal and/or long-term preservation. In the Government of Canada, information is safeguarded as a public trust and managed as a strategic asset to maximize its value in the service of Canadians. Information management policies, directives, standards and guidelines support effective decision making and program delivery in the Government of Canada. These policy instruments apply to all federal government departments and are currently being revised under the Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat’s Policy Reset Initiative.” (Reference)

Information is one of the main assets of an organization, so it must be managed in the same way as human resources and financial resources. Information has value: administrative and operational value (it allows us to do our work), legal value, evidential value and potentially historical value. Information supports our decision making and program execution. Therefore, it must be saved in a convenient, easily accessible and reliable area for those who need it.

By adopting IM best practices, we can reduce the time spent searching for information, prevent employees from using outdated information, enable easier sharing of information, reduce unnecessary (and costly) duplication and minimize the processing costs for Access to Information and Privacy (ATIP) requests. Using IM best practices, we can also improve decision-making at DFO and support risk management and business planning continuity. In addition, we can improve the legal eligibility of files, which protects employees and the Department from potential litigations.

The main activities in information management fall into seven stages: planning, creation, organization, use, retention, disposal and evaluation.

Planning

This step allows us to determine what information will be created or captured, and to ensure that it will be reflected in the departmental file plan.

Creation

This step is when you create or enter new information. When business value information is created, it must be saved in a departmental authorized repository. This means it must be saved where it can be accessed by those who need to be aware of it and where it can be managed throughout its life cycle. This is also when the information's security classification is determined.

Organization

The classification and organization of information is essential so that it can be quickly located. Good information organization is essential because it facilitates the efficient and responsible use of information, including research, recovery, and sharing.

Use

This is the stage where information resources are actually used, shared, disseminated, and reworked in order to deliver programs and services, to make decisions, and to meet transparency requirements.

Retention

This step allows us to apply good recordkeeping practices so that information that is well-preserved, maintained, and protected can remain useful and available today and in the future. DFO Historical Document Resources (Archives) are recorded in our Disposition Authorities (RDAs).

Disposal

This step allows us to routinely eliminate documents that no longer have operational value. This destruction must be done according to the procedures established by the information management teams, in accordance with the retention periods approved by the Department and by Library and Archives Canada (LAC). Good information management through this step also allows the transfer of documents of historical significance to LAC.

IM evaluation

This step allows us to evaluate and improve our information management policies and procedures, such as the periodic review of the classification plan and of the disposition authorities.

The Directive on Information Management Roles and Responsibilities aims to determine the roles and responsibilities of all Government of Canada employees to support their organizations in the effective management of departmental information. You are responsible for managing information in accordance with government policies. The files and documents you work on for your DFO job belong to the Department and are not your personal property.

As an employee, you are responsible for:

  • creating and classifying the paper and electronic files that document DFO's activities;
  • saving files in the Department's corporate memory;
  • exercising due diligence in managing information under your control;
  • managing your files' security and access, and for ensuring that they have the correct security level;
  • creating and following naming conventions approved by your directorate;
  • destroying transitory documents when they are no longer needed;
  • adhering to the Retention and disposition schedule when disposing of permanent records;
  • seeking advice from your IM team in difficult or special cases;
  • reporting information management needs and issues to your manager and, if applicable, to IM functional specialists.

Classification and organization of information

Organizing documents is one of the essential steps in managing information throughout its life cycle, from creation to permanent preservation or destruction. This function can be defined as "a set of logically structured conventions, methods, and rules of procedure allowing the classification of documents in groups or in categories, regardless of their support and age". [1] The classification differs from filling which refers to the physical arrangement of documents and to storage operations.

IM Services Branch recommends the development of function classification plans to logically organize information held by DFO. Functions are linked to work processes deriving from DFO mandates, such as financial resource management, real property management, fisheries management, oceans protection management, and so on.

The official DFO classification plan is being revised as part of the implementation of GCdocs. It will be adjusted following consultations with the directorates. DFO classification plan - high level (version of 2016 under revision)

Best practices

Establish a file classification structure based on the functions and activities of your team:

  • Establish a working group to create the structure;
  • Limit to 3 or 4 levels of folders;
  • Limit the 1st level to 10 folders maximum;
  • Group documents of a similar function under a single folder;
  • Do not create "Miscellaneous, Correspondence, Word documents, etc" folders that are catch-all;
  • Do not create folders with employee names due to frequent personnel movements;
  • Have the folder structure approved by management;
  • Share your operational documents by saving them in the official repository, e. g.: GCdocs, shared network drives, etc (always at the most accurate level);
  • Establish a naming convention for the documents and the folders;
  • Respect the naming convention of the documents and folders of your team;
  • Prefer the use of shortcuts to limit the creation of duplicates;
  • Share documents by sending hypertext links rather than email attachments;
  • Check versions and delete earlier versions when they are no longer needed.

Retention specifications

As part of LAC's department review and validation of disposal authorities, the authorizations used by DFO over the past twelve years to dispose of the information are no longer valid. In order to complete this process, DFO information management team is working closely with LAC to validate and complete a new Departmental Disposition Authority.

What does this mean? It means that until we receive the final validated disposition letter from LAC:

  • You can continue to dispose of transitory information (information that is required for a limited time. Example: copies used for reference, drafts containing minor changes, casual communications, personal documents).
  • You can work with your IM Specialist to dispose files related to common administrative functions such as: Acquisition Services, Material Services, Financial Management, Human Resources Management, Information Technology, Information Management, and Communications Services.
  • You cannot dispose of operational information of business value (information required by the Department to control, support, and document program delivery; or that provides proof of decisions and transactions).
  • You can work with your IM Specialist to transfer information of enduring value to LAC if the information was created prior to 1946 or in some instances, where information is related to Aboriginal Consultations/Matters. All other information of enduring value cannot be transferred to LAC at this time.

Best practices in paper information management Here is a list of good practices for managing paper documents and files:

  • Keep only original and business value information in your files and clean-up regularly your transitory information, which include:
    • Copies of documents used for reference;
    • Notes or “to-do” lists on paper;
    • Printed versions of drafts that include minor changes or spelling mistakes;
    • Informal communications;
    • Printed versions of documents (e. g.: reports) that have been created from a database and can be recreated at any time;
    • Personal documents related to work (curriculum vitae, cover letters, etc.);
    • Personal information (photos, correspondance, etc).
  • Respect the same classification structure used for electronic documents :
    • Group paper documents related to the same function in a file;
    • Identify your paper files with function or activity described in your classification plan;
    • Be clear in the identification of your paper files: you are the expert, the person who will manage your paper files later cannot guess or interpret whether those are business value or in what context they have been created.
  • Avoid printing electronic documents unnecessarily: this way you can better manage versions, reduce duplicates and help reduce hybrid documents.
  • Do not destroy business value documents without the authorization of IM.

Regional IM teams also provide document shredding supports to their clients. All costs associated to shredding, including acquisition and maintenance of shredders, are subject to cost recovery.

  • It is your responsibility to clean-up your paper files:
    • Destroy transitory information;
    • Ask your IM specialist if you need boxes for the destruction of paper documents;
    • Keep in mind that information (both paper and electronic supports) doesn’t belong to you: in fact, as a Government employee, records you create as part of your business functions belong to the department, and by extension, to all Canadians.

Digitization for substitution

Even if most of documents are created electronically, it is also possible to have hybrid documents: this is the case, for example, when an electronic document is printed or when a paper document is digitalized for the purpose of replacing the original.

If you want to digitalized original documents on paper support for the purpose of substitution (which means destroy the original paper to only keep the digitalized version), know this:

  • You must comply with the CAN/CGSB-72.34-2017 standard, which governs electronic evidence, as well as authenticity and integrity of documents when scanning source document;
  • The following types of documents cannot be destroyed after their digitalization:
    • Documents created before 1939;
    • Proclamations, charts, declarations, intergovernmental agreements, and original treaties;
    • Documents bearing an official seal;
    • Large graphic documents;
    • Cartographic and architectural documents or engineering drawings;
    • Photographs, slides and negatives and;
    • Original artistic creations.
  • The Canada Evidence Act also controls the authenticity and integrity of electronic documents for evidentiary purposes: under section 31.1, “Any person seeking to admit an electronic document as evidence has the burden of proving its authenticity by evidence capable of supporting a finding that the electronic document is that which it is purported to be."[1] If in doubt, do not destroy anything and contact your IM specialist.
  • Multi-Institutional Authorization No. 2015-011 of Library and Archives Canada (LAC) authorizes the destruction of government source documents after they are digitized.

The naming convention is a tool that will aid your business team to appropriately name their documents. The purpose of the naming convention is to establish a common and uniform approach to all employees of a business branch for the naming of their electronic documents. Its adoption will allow to improve spotting, research, and information sharing. This will also avoid the loss of information, especially during staff mobility, as well as unnecessary production and recording of existing information.

Once the naming convention is adopted, it is the responsibility of everyone to adequately name documents they created or received from the first entry. If a received document is not adequately named, you must rename it when you save it. Identify each electronic document with the appropriate title, exhaustive and representative of its content. This way, you will no longer need to open it to be sure that it is the right document and the right version.

Computer network and shared network drives

***Photo de réseau informatique

A computer network is defined as a set of computers connected to each other to exchange and share information. Among these computer equipments, the computers that hold the shared resources are called servers. For example, a file server allows you to share data and information across the network. The common storage space is then called network drive. There are personal network drives (U :) whose access is limited to a user, and shared network drives for user groups. In the fall of 2017, documents and data from DFO teams are stored on hundreds of servers across the country.

All Government of Canada’s shared network drives, including those of DFO, are under the responsibility of Shared Services Canada (SSC), which hosts these servers in data centers. As of April 1st, 2017, SSC was managing 20,000 computer servers. Under the Data Centre Consolidation Program, that is running from 2017 to 2024, SSC will:

  • consolidate approximately 700 legacy data centres spread out across the country to seven or fewer in Ontario, Quebec and possibly western Canada ;
  • reduce data centre space across Canada by 60 percent, from 56,000 m2 to 17,000 m2 ;
  • reduce the Government of Canada carbon footprint by 40 percent by 2024-2025, by reducing data centre space and building and/or leasing state-of-the-art data centres with built-in green technology and energy efficiency ;
  • leverage faster, better, greener and more secure technology to increase security against physical and cyber threats.
(Source : Shared Services Canada)

Best practices on shared network drives

Here is a list of good practices to do when working on shared network drives:

  • Comply with the DFO's Standard on the Storage of Electronic Information
    • For personal network drives;
    • For shared network drives;
    • For electronic document management systems (EKME, GCdocs);
    • For e-mail systems (e-mail, instant messaging, etc.
  • Do not save anything to your computer's hard drive (usually named OS-Disk C :). The contents of your computer's hard drive is not included in the daily backup operations of shared network drives. In addition, documents stored in the internal hard disk of your computer desk cannot be shared.
  • Establish a file classification structure based on the functions and activities of your team:
    • Form a working group to create the structure and establish the names of the directories and folders;
    • Limit to 3 or 4 levels the number of directories and subdirectories;
    • Limit the 1st level to 10 directories maximum;
    • At each level of the structure, create directories of similar category;
    • Do not create folders with employee names due to frequent staff movements.
  • Your structure must be approved by the direction;
  • Share your operational documents by saving them in the appropriate folders on shared network drives (always at the most accurate level);
  • Establish a naming convention for the documents;
  • Respect your team’s naming convention for the documents;
  • Prefer the use of shortcuts to limit the creation of duplicates;
  • Share documents by sending hypertext links rather than email attachments;
  • Check versions and delete earlier versions when they are no longer needed;
  • Regularly clean transitory documents like:
    • Copies of documents used for reference;
    • Notes written to prepare the layout of a document;
    • Provisional notes or “to-do” lists
    • Drafts that include minor changes and grammatical corrections;
    • Informal communications (dinner, IT updates, mass notifications, etc.);
    • Documents (eg: reports) that have been created from a database and can be recreated at any time;
    • Personal documents related to work (curriculum vitae and cover letters, etc.);
    • Personal information (photos, music, etc.);
    • Duplicates.
  • Do not destroy business value documents without the authorization of IM.
How do I clean up shared network drives?

In order to help you to better manage your digital workspaces, the IM team can provide you with different types of report about the network drives to which you have access. These reports are produced using the File Reporter system based on information provided by Shared Services Canada (SSC). They can be sent to you in different formats (.pdf, .xslx, .csv, .rtf, .txt).

You want ... Answer/Solution
You want reports about your shared network drives? Contact the IM team by email specifying the address of the path and the name of the drive concerned (for example: dcqcquena01_Groupe_BIESS)
You want to know what your shared network drives contain? Ask us for summary reports and filename extensions reports.
You want to free up space on your shared network drives? Ask us for duplicates reports, filename extensions reports or reports on the date of last access to documents.
Want to know which are the biggest consumers of space on your shared drives? Ask us for owner reports.
Want to verify that proper use is made of shared network drives? Ask us for filename extensions reports.
Duplicate report ***Rapport doublons.jpg

The duplicate report is the essential tool for any clean-up operation in shared network drives. It helps identify and group different copies of a document. The system identifies potential duplicates by identifying documents that have the same name and size. It also specifies the name of the files, their location, their size, their last date of access, the date of last modification, the owner, the date of creation and the author. To establish who the document’s owner is, File Reporter follows the Windows Owner metadata that considers the last modifier of a document as its owner. Author metadata is the one that appears in the Document Properties of the Office suite. If it is empty, the system takes the name of the last modifier.

The duplicate report is provided in Excel format.

Summary report ***Rapport sommaire.jpg

The summary report gives you an overview of your drive’s structure (folders of 1st, 2nd, 3rd and 4th level). It presents a summary of existing folders on a drive or on a part of a drive. For each identified folder, it specifies the folder’s path, its size, and its number of subfolders and files. That type of report also indicates the 10 biggest folders found.

Owner report

The owner report presents information related to owners of documents. For each identified person, it specifies the identification network name of the person (last name + 1st letter of the first name), and the total number and size of files that they owned. To determine who the document’s owner is, File Reporter follows Windows Owner metadata that considers the last modifier of a document as its owner. This kind of report also identifies the 10 users who use the most space.

Detailed owner report

The detailed owner report presents information related to one specific owner. It details, for each folder, the files name, their size, their date of creation, their date of modification, and their last date of access.

Filename extension report ***Rapport extension de fichier.jpg

The filename extension report presents information about the types of extensions that appear in the drive or in part of the drive targeted. It allows you to identify the 10 extension that take the most space. For each identified filename extension, the report indicates the volume of related documents and the number of files. It can help you target certain types of files during clean-up operations on shared network drives.

Detailed filename extension report

The detailed filename extension presents information related to a specific extension. For each folder, it details filenames, their size, their owner, their creation date, their date of modification and the last date of access.

Date-Age report

This report indicates the number and volume of the files in terms of their creation date, their date of modification or their last date of access (by day, month or year).

Date-Age detailed report

This report presents information related to a specific date (e. g. : accessed documents for the last time in 2012, documents created in March 2015, etc.). For each folder, it details the filenames, their size, their owner, their creation date, their modification date, and their last date of access.
